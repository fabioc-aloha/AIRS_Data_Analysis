### **1. Dilution Effect Theory** (Novel Contribution)

**Core Proposition**: Heterogeneous sample composition prevents context-specific psychological constructs from emerging psychometrically, regardless of sample size or internal consistency reliability.

**Theoretical Foundations**:
- **Social Identity Theory** (Tajfel & Turner, 1979): Heterogeneous samples activate superordinate shared identity, suppressing subgroup-specific schemas
- **Faultline Theory** (Lau & Murnighan, 1998): Demographic divisions fragment psychological cohesion, preventing distinctive attitudes from crystallizing
- **Diversity Research** (Harrison & Klein, 2007): Separation/variety/disparity forms of diversity mask within-group dynamics
- **Schema Theory** (Fiske & Taylor, 1991): Context determines which cognitive schemas activate; heterogeneity activates lowest-common-denominator schemas

**Empirical Evidence Pattern**:
```

â”œâ”€ Cronbach's Î± = 0.962 (excellent reliability)
â”œâ”€ HTMT = 0.979 (failed discriminant validity, <0.85 threshold)
â””â”€ Interpretation: Resistance signal diluted by 38.5% students + 6.9% faculty

â”œâ”€ Cronbach's Î±â‚ = 0.967 (excellent reliability)
â”œâ”€ HTMT < 0.85 (passed discriminant validity)
â””â”€ Interpretation: Homogeneity allows resistance construct to emerge
```

**Mechanism**:
1. Heterogeneous contexts â†’ Superordinate schema activation ("general tech user")
2. Subgroup schemas suppressed ("professional evaluator", "student explorer")
3. Weighted average produces consensus constructs (readiness)
4. Distinctive concerns (resistance) diluted despite presence in majority subgroup

**Novel Implications**:
- **Sample composition > sample size** for multidimensional construct emergence
- **Reliability alone insufficient** for construct validity (Î±â‰ discriminant validity)
- **Measurement invariance testing incomplete** without testing dimensionality itself
- **Generalizability paradox**: Universal samples may mask context-specific psychology

### **2. Context-Contingent Dimensionality Framework** (Novel Contribution)

**Core Proposition**: Technology adoption psychology exhibits context-dependent dimensionality where factor structure itself (not just relationships or means) varies fundamentally across populations.

**Paradigm Challenge**:
- **Traditional Moderation Framework** (Baron & Kenny, 1986): Same constructs, different slopes

- **Context-Contingent Framework**: Different constructs entirely across contexts
  - Context A: {X, Y}
  - Context B: {X, Y, Z}
  - Context C: {X, W}
  - Factor structure itself is context-dependent

### **4. UTAUT2 Boundary Conditions Framework** (Extension)

**Core Proposition**: Technology acceptance constructs exhibit tiered universalityâ€”some transcend context (universal), others require homogeneity (context-sensitive), others require all three emergence conditions (professional-specific).

#### **Tier 1: Universal Core** (Present in All 3 Models)

**Constructs**:
- **Performance Expectancy** (PE1, PE2): "Will this help me accomplish tasks?"
- **Hedonic Motivation** (HM1, HM2): "Is this enjoyable to use?"
- **Price Value** (PV1, PV2): "Is this worth the cost/effort?"

**Theoretical Foundation**:
- Core UTAUT2 (Venkatesh et al., 2012): Consumer context extensions
- Fundamental cost-benefit evaluation (rational choice theory)
- Cross-cultural replication (Dwivedi et al., 2019)

**Interpretation**: These represent **baseline technology evaluation dimensions** that transcend context, experience, and demographicsâ€”human universal cognitive processes.

#### **Tier 2: Context-Sensitive Extensions** (Subsamples Only)

**Constructs**:
- **Habit** (HB1, HB2): Subsamples only (not in heterogeneous full sample)
- **Trust** (TR1, TR2): Subsamples only (verification becomes salient with experience)
- **Voluntariness** (VO1): Subsamples only (autonomy concerns emerge with identity)
- **Social Influence** (SI1): **Professionals** (workplace norms) vs. **Students** (individual choice)
- **Facilitating Conditions** (FC): Full + Students but **different items** (resource concerns context-specific)
- **Effort Expectancy** (EE): Full sample only (heterogeneous contexts highlight ease of use)

**Theoretical Foundation**:
- Habit requires behavioral history (Limayem et al., 2007)
- Trust emerges with experience (McKnight et al., 2002)
- Voluntariness salient when autonomy threatened (Deci & Ryan, 2000)
- Social influence depends on reference group (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000)

**Interpretation**: These constructs require **homogeneous contexts** to emerge consistentlyâ€”heterogeneity activates different facets for different subgroups, preventing clean psychometric emergence.

#### **Tier 3: Professional-Specific Constructs** (Requires All 3 Emergence Conditions)

**Constructs**:
- **Anxiety** (AX1): "I feel apprehensive about using AI tools"
- **Effort Resistance** (ER2): "Learning AI tools requires too much effort"

**Theoretical Foundation**:
- Dual-process theories (Kahneman, 2011): Type 2 systematic processing required
- Ambivalence research (Priester & Petty, 1996): Simultaneous positive/negative evaluations
- Career identity threat (Bandura, 1997): Professional self-efficacy at stake

**Evidence**:
- **Professionals**: r=-0.296 (readiness-resistance) = ambivalence
- **Students**: Unidimensional (no ambivalence, 95% enthusiasm)
- **Full sample**: Resistance diluted despite 54.7% professionals

**Interpretation**: Resistance is not suppressed or weak in studentsâ€”**it does not exist psychologically**. Requires all three conditions (experience + homogeneity + stakes) to activate.
